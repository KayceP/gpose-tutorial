Rule of Thirds: The Foundation

Why It Works

Our eyes naturally scan images in patterns. Placing subjects at intersection points creates visual tension that feels dynamic and interesting‚Äîcentering feels static and "safe."

Placement Guide

  • Portraits: Place eyes at upper intersection points
  • Full body: Character at left or right third line
  • Action shots: Leave space in the direction of movement
  • Landscapes: Horizon on upper or lower third, never center

Portrait: Eyes on upper-left intersection

Landscape: Horizon on lower third

GPose Tip: Use camera pan (W/A/S/D) to shift your subject to intersections after framing‚Äîdon't move your character! Also try Ctrl+Arrow keys for fine adjustments.
The rule of thirds is the single most important composition technique. Never center your subject unless it's intentional for symmetry. For portraits, eyes should be at the upper intersections - this is where viewers naturally look first. Leave "breathing room" - if your character faces left, place them on the right third so they have space to "look into." The psychological reason: centered images feel "complete" and our eye stops. Off-center creates tension that keeps viewers engaged.

GPose Lighting Panel Mastery

üí° Light Types 1, 2, 3

  • Type 1: Key light - your main light source, brightest
  • Type 2: Fill light - softer, reduces harsh shadows
  • Type 3: Rim/accent light - separates subject from background

üé® Color Recommendations

  • Skin-friendly: Warm white (255, 244, 229) or soft orange
  • Moonlight: Pale blue (200, 220, 255)
  • Dramatic: Complementary colors on opposite sides
Character Lighting Toggle: Turn OFF to let custom lights fully control the scene. Turn ON to keep zone lighting on character.
The three-point lighting setup is a film industry standard: key light at 45 degrees, fill light opposite and softer, rim light behind. For portraits, a slightly warm key light is universally flattering - pure white can look harsh. The Character Lighting toggle is crucial - when it's on, the game's default lighting still affects your character. Turn it off for full control.

Depth of Field: The Portrait Secret

f/1.4 - f/2.8

Maximum blur, dreamy portraits, subject isolation

f/4 - f/5.6

Balanced blur, group shots, environmental portraits

f/8 - f/16

Sharp throughout, landscapes, architecture

GPose DoF Settings Explained

Focus Distance: How far the sharp focus point is. Click your subject or adjust manually.
F-Stop (Aperture): Lower = more blur. f/1.4 is maximum blur, f/16 is minimal.
Focal Length: Affects blur quality. Higher values = creamier bokeh but narrower view.
Quick Recipe: For a classic portrait with blurred background, use f/2.0, focus on eyes, focal length 85-135mm.

Example: f/2.0, eyes in focus, 100mm focal length

Depth of field is what makes professional photos look professional. The background blur draws all attention to your subject. In real photography, f-stop controls how much light enters AND depth of field. In GPose, it only affects blur. Lower f-stop numbers mean wider aperture = more blur. It's counterintuitive but memorize: small number = big blur. For portraits: f/1.4-2.8. For group shots where everyone needs to be sharp: f/5.6-8. For landscapes: f/11-16.

What is ReShade?

Before
After

ReShade is a post-processing injector that adds visual effects to your game in real-time.

Key Shaders Explained:

  • MXAO: Ambient occlusion - adds depth shadows in crevices
  • DOF: Advanced depth of field with bokeh shapes
  • Clarity: Enhances local contrast and detail
  • LUT: Color lookup tables for color grading
  • Bloom: Glowing highlights, light bleed effects
‚ö†Ô∏è Safety: ReShade is visual-only and has never caused bans. Don't discuss tools in-game.
ReShade processes each frame after FFXIV renders it, adding effects on top. MXAO is the biggest visual upgrade - it adds realistic shadowing that makes everything look more 3D. DOF in ReShade is more advanced than GPose's built-in - you get bokeh shape control and better blur quality. LUTs are like Instagram filters but professional - they can completely change the mood of a shot.
